# Chinese Scientists Achieve Room-Temperature Quantum Computing Breakthrough

**Source:** Science  
**Author:** Dr. Li Wei  
**Date:** April 18, 2025

## Summary
Researchers at Beijing University successfully demonstrate stable quantum computing operations at room temperature, potentially revolutionizing the industry by eliminating expensive cooling requirements and making quantum computers accessible worldwide.

## News Report

Scientists at Beijing University today announced a revolutionary breakthrough in quantum computing, successfully demonstrating stable quantum operations at room temperature for over 72 hours. The achievement, published in Science, could eliminate the need for expensive cooling systems that have limited quantum computers to specialized laboratories, potentially making quantum computing accessible to businesses and researchers worldwide.

The breakthrough utilizes a novel approach involving synthetic diamond matrices and precisely controlled electromagnetic fields to maintain quantum coherence without the ultra-low temperatures typically required. Traditional quantum computers operate at temperatures near absolute zero, requiring cooling systems that cost millions of dollars and consume enormous amounts of energy.

"This changes everything about quantum computing," explained Dr. Li Wei, lead researcher on the project. "We can now build quantum computers that operate in normal office environments, making this technology accessible to universities, small businesses, and developing countries that could never afford traditional quantum systems."

The room-temperature quantum processor demonstrated in Beijing can perform calculations equivalent to current supercooled systems while consuming 99% less energy. The technology uses abundant materials and manufacturing processes that could reduce quantum computer costs from $15 million to under $100,000, potentially triggering widespread adoption across industries.

International technology companies have responded with alarm and excitement, with several announcing emergency research initiatives to replicate the Chinese breakthrough. The development could shift global quantum computing leadership to China, which has invested heavily in quantum research over the past decade.

The implications extend far beyond computing, as room-temperature quantum systems could revolutionize cryptography, drug discovery, financial modeling, and artificial intelligence. However, the breakthrough also raises security concerns, as widespread quantum computing access could threaten current encryption systems that protect global communications and financial transactions.

Chinese officials have indicated they plan to commercialize the technology rapidly, with the first room-temperature quantum computers expected to reach market within two years, potentially giving Chinese companies a significant advantage in quantum-powered industries.
